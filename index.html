Electrostatics: Fundamental Concepts

What is Electrostatics?

Electrostatics is the study of electric charges at rest. Unlike current electricity which deals with moving charges, electrostatics focuses on stationary charges and the forces, fields, and potentials they create. This forms the foundation for understanding capacitors, dielectrics, and many electrical devices.

Key Concept: Two Types of Charges

There exist two fundamental types of electric charge: positive (proton) and negative (electron). The fundamental law: Like charges repel, unlike charges attract. The elementary charge is $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C.

MDCAT/NUMS Focus Areas:

  • Charge quantization and conservation problems
  • Gold leaf electroscope working principle
  • Methods of charging (friction, conduction, induction)
  • Solving problems with multiple charged objects

Imaginary Cases for Competition Exams:

  1. Charge on a moving ring: If a uniformly charged ring rotates about its axis, the charge density remains constant but current is created.
  2. Charge distribution on irregular shapes: On conductors, charge density is highest at sharp points (lightning rod principle).
  3. Charge in non-uniform fields: A dipole in non-uniform field experiences both torque and net force.
Exceptional Case: Charge Shielding

Faraday cage effect: In a hollow conductor, external electric fields cannot penetrate the interior. This is why cars are safe during lightning and sensitive equipment is placed in metal enclosures. The electric field inside a hollow conductor is zero regardless of external field strength.

$\text{Charge Quantization: } Q = \pm ne \quad \text{where } n = 1, 2, 3, \dots$

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Charge is conserved in all physical processes
  2. Charge is quantized in multiples of $e$
  3. Charge is invariant (doesn't change with velocity)
  4. Charge is additive (total charge = algebraic sum)

Conceptual Understanding (3/30)

Why can't we have a fractional electric charge in isolation?
Answer: Due to charge quantization principle. All observable charges are integer multiples of the elementary charge $e = 1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ C. Quarks have fractional charges ($\pm\frac{1}{3}e, \pm\frac{2}{3}e$) but are always confined within hadrons by the strong nuclear force, so isolated fractional charges are not observed in nature.
How does a gold leaf electroscope detect charge?
Answer: When a charged object touches the metal cap, charge distributes over the electroscope. The metal stem and gold leaves acquire like charges, causing electrostatic repulsion between them. The angle of divergence indicates the amount of charge. For induction detection: bringing a charged rod near causes temporary charge separation, touching the cap while rod is near grounds it, removing the ground then rod leaves it permanently charged.
What happens when a charged insulator is brought near an uncharged conductor?
Answer: Charge induction occurs. Electrons in the conductor redistribute: opposite charge accumulates near the insulator, like charge moves away. If the conductor is grounded while the insulator is near, electrons flow to/from ground, leaving the conductor with net charge opposite to the insulator when ground connection is removed first, then insulator removed.

Electric Charge: Properties and Measurement

Properties of Electric Charge:

  1. Quantization: $Q = ne$, where $n = \pm1, \pm2, \pm3, \dots$
  2. Conservation: Total charge in isolated system is constant
  3. Additivity: Total charge = algebraic sum of individual charges
  4. Invariance: Charge is independent of frame of reference
Key Concept: Charge Invariance

Unlike mass, electric charge is invariant under Lorentz transformations. A charged particle has the same charge regardless of its speed. This is a fundamental postulate of relativity confirmed by experiments. Mass increases with velocity, but charge remains constant.

Charge Density Concepts:

$\text{Linear: } \lambda = \frac{Q}{L} \quad \text{Surface: } \sigma = \frac{Q}{A} \quad \text{Volume: } \rho = \frac{Q}{V}$

HEC/AKU Level Problems:

  • Charge distribution on irregular conductors
  • Charge transfer between identical/different spheres
  • Charge conservation in nuclear reactions
  • Millikan's oil drop experiment calculations
Exceptional Case: Charge on Sharp Points

On a conductor, charge density is inversely proportional to radius of curvature. Sharp points have very high charge density, leading to corona discharge. This principle is used in lightning rods to safely discharge clouds and in electrostatic precipitators to remove dust particles.

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. 1 Coulomb = charge of $6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons
  2. Electron charge: $-1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C
  3. Proton charge: $+1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C
  4. Neutron charge: 0 (electrically neutral)

Conceptual Understanding (6/30)

Why does charge accumulate at sharp points on a conductor?
Answer: Due to the inverse relationship between charge density and radius of curvature. For a conductor at constant potential $V$, surface charge density $\sigma = \epsilon_0 E$. Electric field $E$ is maximum at points with smallest radius of curvature (sharp points). Thus $\sigma$ is highest at sharp points, leading to intense electric fields that can ionize air molecules causing corona discharge.
Can a body have charge without mass?
Answer: No. All known charged particles (electrons, protons, ions) have mass. Photons are massless but carry no electric charge. In quantum field theory, charge is a property of particles with mass (except hypothetical magnetic monopoles which haven't been observed). Charge carriers require mass to exist as particles.
Why is charge considered quantized but continuous in macroscopic physics?
Answer: At microscopic level, charge comes in discrete packets of $e = 1.6 \times 10^{-19}$ C. However, macroscopic objects contain billions of electrons, so charge appears continuous. For example, 1 C contains $6.25 \times 10^{18}$ electrons, making step changes imperceptible, allowing us to treat charge as continuous in circuit theory.

Coulomb's Law: Force Between Charges

Coulomb's Law Equation:

$\vec{F} = k \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} \quad \text{where } k = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0} = 9 \times 10^9 \text{ N·m}^2/\text{C}^2$
Key Concept: Vector Nature of Coulomb Force

Coulomb force is a vector quantity. For multiple charges, use vector addition: $\vec{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum \vec{F}_i$. The principle of superposition applies: force between any two charges is unaffected by the presence of other charges. Always calculate forces separately then add vectorially.

Coulomb's Law in Material Media:

$\vec{F}_{\text{medium}} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} = \frac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0\epsilon_r} \frac{q_1 q_2}{r^2} \hat{r} = \frac{\vec{F}_{\text{vacuum}}}{\epsilon_r}$

Effect Analysis (Competition Focus):

If $F = k\frac{q_1q_2}{r^2}$, then:

  • If $q_1$ doubles: $F$ doubles (linear relationship)
  • If $r$ halves: $F$ quadruples (inverse square law)
  • If medium changes from air ($\epsilon_r \approx 1$) to water ($\epsilon_r \approx 80$): $F$ reduces to $\frac{1}{80}$
  • If both charges reverse sign: $F$ direction reverses but magnitude unchanged
Exceptional Case: Comparing Coulomb and Gravitational Forces

For two protons: $\frac{F_e}{F_g} = \frac{kq^2}{Gm^2} \approx 1.24 \times 10^{36}$. Electrostatic force is enormously stronger than gravity at atomic scales. This explains why chemical bonds (electrostatic) dominate over gravitational attraction in molecules. Gravity becomes significant only for astronomical masses.

MDCAT/NUMS Problem Solving Strategy:

  1. Convert all units to SI (Coulombs, meters)
  2. Use $k = 9 \times 10^9$ N·m²/C²
  3. For symmetrical arrangements, use symmetry to cancel components
  4. For equilibrium problems, set net force = 0

Conceptual Understanding (12/30)

Why is Coulomb's law called an inverse square law?
Answer: Because force varies inversely with square of distance: $F \propto 1/r^2$. This arises from geometry: electric field lines spread uniformly in all directions over sphere surface area $4\pi r^2$, so flux density $\propto 1/r^2$. Similar to gravity and light intensity. If it were $1/r^3$, field lines wouldn't conserve; if $1/r$, they'd converge.
Does Coulomb's law hold for charges in motion?
Answer: No. Coulomb's law is strictly for stationary charges. For moving charges, magnetic fields are generated and the complete electromagnetic interaction is described by Lorentz force law: $\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B})$. At low speeds ($v \ll c$), Coulomb's law is approximately valid, but relativistic corrections are needed as $v$ approaches light speed.
What happens to Coulomb force if dielectric constant increases?
Answer: Force decreases inversely with dielectric constant: $F_{\text{medium}} = F_{\text{vacuum}}/\epsilon_r$. Dielectric material polarizes, creating bound charges that produce opposing electric field reducing net field between charges. In water ($\epsilon_r \approx 80$), force is only 1.25% of vacuum value, explaining why salt dissolves easily.
Why can we use superposition principle for Coulomb forces?
Answer: Because electric force is linear in charge. The force on a charge due to multiple charges is vector sum of forces from each charge individually. This works because electric fields add vectorially and $F = qE$. Superposition fails only in extreme conditions (very high fields causing non-linear media or quantum mechanical effects).
What is the range of Coulomb force?
Answer: Coulomb force has infinite range in theory ($1/r^2$ never becomes zero). However, in practice, it's effectively shielded in conductors (screened by mobile charges) and neutralized in bulk matter by opposite charges. In vacuum, it extends infinitely but becomes immeasurably small at large distances. Compare with nuclear force (range $10^{-15}$ m) and gravity (infinite range).
How does Coulomb's law verify Newton's third law?
Answer: Coulomb force between two charges is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction: $\vec{F}_{12} = -\vec{F}_{21}$. This satisfies Newton's third law. The forces act along the line joining the charges. This holds true even if charges are moving slowly, but for rapidly moving charges, magnetic forces complicate the picture.

Permittivity & Dielectrics

Relative Permittivity (Dielectric Constant):

$\epsilon_r = \frac{\epsilon}{\epsilon_0} = \frac{C_{\text{medium}}}{C_{\text{vacuum}}} = \frac{F_{\text{vacuum}}}{F_{\text{medium}}}$

Where $\epsilon_r \geq 1$ for all materials. It measures how much a material reduces electric field compared to vacuum.

Key Concept: Polarization Mechanism

Dielectrics reduce electric field through polarization: 1) Electronic polarization (electron cloud distortion), 2) Ionic polarization (ion displacement in ionic crystals), 3) Orientation polarization (dipole alignment in polar molecules). Total polarization $P = \epsilon_0\chi_e E$, where $\chi_e$ is electric susceptibility.

Competition Exam Tricks:

  1. If dielectric fills half the space between charges, effective $\epsilon_r$ is average of two regions
  2. For dielectric slab between charges, force reduces by $\epsilon_r$ only if slab fills entire space
  3. Dielectric breakdown occurs when $E > E_{\text{max}}$ for material
  4. $\epsilon_r$ for water is 80 but reduces to 1.8 at optical frequencies
Exceptional Case: Frequency Dependence

$\epsilon_r$ decreases with increasing frequency. At low frequencies, all polarization mechanisms work. At radio frequencies, dipoles can't follow field (orientation polarization stops). At optical frequencies, only electronic polarization remains. This explains why water $\epsilon_r$ drops from 80 (DC) to 1.8 (visible light).

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Vacuum permittivity: $\epsilon_0 = 8.85 \times 10^{-12}$ C²/N·m²
  2. $\epsilon_r$ (air) ≈ 1.0006 (usually taken as 1)
  3. $\epsilon_r$ (water) ≈ 80
  4. $\epsilon_r$ (glass) ≈ 5-10
  5. $\epsilon_r$ (paper) ≈ 3-4

Conceptual Understanding (15/30)

Why does force between charges decrease in a dielectric medium?
Answer: Dielectric polarizes, creating bound charges on surfaces. These bound charges produce an internal field $E_p$ opposing the external field $E_0$. Net field $E = E_0 - E_p = E_0/\epsilon_r$, so force $F = qE = qE_0/\epsilon_r = F_0/\epsilon_r$. Bound charges partially cancel the free charges' effect, reducing net force.
Can relative permittivity be less than 1?
Answer: Normally no for ordinary materials, as $\epsilon_r = 1 + \chi_e$ and electric susceptibility $\chi_e \geq 0$. However, in metamaterials and plasma at certain frequencies, effective $\epsilon_r$ can be negative, enabling unusual effects like negative refraction and perfect lensing. Also, in ferroelectric materials near phase transition, $\epsilon_r$ can be very high (>1000).
What is the difference between dielectric constant and dielectric strength?
Answer: Dielectric constant ($\epsilon_r$) measures polarization ability (how much it reduces electric field). Dielectric strength measures maximum electric field before breakdown (ionization). Example: Air has $\epsilon_r \approx 1$, strength = 3 MV/m; Transformer oil: $\epsilon_r \approx 2.2$, strength = 15 MV/m. A good dielectric needs high $\epsilon_r$ AND high strength.

Electric Field: Concepts and Applications

Electric Field Intensity:

$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q_0} \quad \text{or} \quad E = \frac{kq}{r^2} \ \text{(point charge)}$

Electric field is a vector field representing force per unit charge. Units: N/C or V/m.

Key Concept: Electric Field Lines

Electric field lines: 1) Start on positive charges, end on negative charges, 2) Density proportional to field strength, 3) Never intersect, 4) Direction is tangent to field, 5) Perpendicular to conductor surfaces. Number of lines from charge $q$ is proportional to $q$.

Electric Field for Various Charges:

$\text{Point charge: } E = \frac{kq}{r^2}$
$\text{Infinite line charge: } E = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\epsilon_0 r}$
$\text{Infinite sheet: } E = \frac{\sigma}{2\epsilon_0}$
$\text{Between parallel plates: } E = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}$

Electric Field in Material Media:

$\vec{D} = \epsilon_0\vec{E} + \vec{P} = \epsilon\vec{E} = \epsilon_0\epsilon_r\vec{E}$

Where $\vec{D}$ is electric displacement field (C/m²), independent of medium. $\vec{P}$ is polarization density.

Exceptional Case: Field Inside Conductor

In electrostatic equilibrium, electric field inside conductor is zero. Any initial field causes charge redistribution until internal field cancels. Field exists only at surface, normal to surface, with magnitude $E = \sigma/\epsilon_0$. This is why hollow conductors shield interiors (Faraday cage).

Direct MCQ Points:

  1. Field due to point charge: radial, $E \propto 1/r^2$
  2. Field due to infinite line: radial, $E \propto 1/r$
  3. Field due to infinite sheet: uniform, constant
  4. Field inside spherical shell: zero
  5. Field inside solid charged sphere: $E \propto r$

Conceptual Understanding (30/30)

Why are electric field lines perpendicular to conductor surface?
Answer: Because conductor surface is equipotential. If field had tangential component, it would do work moving charge along surface, changing potential. Since $E = -\nabla V$, and $V$ is constant on surface, $\nabla V$ has no tangential component, so $E$ is purely normal. Any tangential component would cause current flow until surface becomes equipotential.
What is the physical significance of electric field lines density?
Answer: Number of field lines per unit area crossing normally is proportional to electric field magnitude. If 10 lines cross 1 m² at point A and 40 lines cross 1 m² at point B (same orientation), then $E_B = 4E_A$. This is a visualization tool - lines aren't physically real but help visualize field strength and direction. More lines = stronger field.
Can electric field exist in a conductor in electrostatic equilibrium?
Answer: No. In electrostatic equilibrium, electric field inside conductor is zero. Any initial field causes charge redistribution until internal field cancels. Field exists only at surface, normal to surface, with magnitude $E = \sigma/\epsilon_0$. If field existed inside, free electrons would move until they cancel it (takes ~$10^{-19}$ s in copper).
What happens to electric field if all dimensions of a system are doubled?
Answer: Depends on charge distribution. For point charge: $E \propto 1/r^2$, so at doubled distance, field becomes 1/4. For infinite line: $E \propto 1/r$, becomes 1/2. For infinite plane: $E$ constant, unchanged. For dipole: $E \propto 1/r^3$, becomes 1/8. For spherical charge: inside sphere $E \propto r$, doubles; outside $E \propto 1/r^2$, becomes 1/4.
Why can't electric field lines form closed loops in electrostatics?
Answer: Because electrostatic field is conservative: $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = 0$. If field lines formed closed loop, integrating along loop would give nonzero work done per unit charge, violating conservativeness. In changing magnetic fields (electrodynamics), field lines can form closed loops ($\nabla \times \vec{E} \neq 0$).
What is the difference between electric field and electric flux?
Answer: Electric field ($\vec{E}$) is vector quantity (force per unit charge) at a point. Electric flux ($\Phi_E$) is scalar quantity (field lines passing through area): $\Phi_E = \vec{E} \cdot \vec{A} = EA\cos\theta$. Flux measures "flow" of field through surface. Gauss's law: total flux through closed surface = $q_{\text{enc}}/\epsilon_0$.
How does electric field inside dielectric compare to external field?
Answer: Electric field inside dielectric is reduced: $E = E_0/\epsilon_r$, where $E_0$ is external field in vacuum. Dielectric polarization creates bound charges producing opposing field. However, displacement field $\vec{D} = \epsilon_0\epsilon_r\vec{E}$ remains continuous across boundaries if no free charges. In capacitors, dielectric reduces voltage for same charge or increases charge for same voltage.
What is zero field point and how to find it?
Answer: Zero field point is where net electric field is zero. For two point charges $q_1$ and $q_2$ separated by $d$, along line joining them: $kq_1/x^2 = kq_2/(d-x)^2$. Solve for $x$. For like charges, zero point lies between them if charges equal, closer to smaller charge if unequal. For unlike charges, zero point lies outside line, closer to smaller charge magnitude.
Why does electric field become infinite at point charge?
Answer: Mathematically, $E = kq/r^2 \to \infty$ as $r \to 0$. Physically, point charge is idealization - real charges have finite size. At atomic distances (~$10^{-10}$ m), quantum effects dominate and classical electrostatics breaks down. Also, measuring field arbitrarily close to charge is impossible practically. The infinity indicates singularity in theory at $r=0$.
What are properties of electric field intensity?
Answer: 1) Vector quantity with magnitude and direction. 2) Conservative: $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{l} = 0$. 3) Obeys superposition: $\vec{E}_{\text{total}} = \sum \vec{E}_i$. 4) Field lines never intersect (unique direction at each point). 5) Proportional to force on test charge: $\vec{F} = q\vec{E}$. 6) Continuous except at point charges or surface charges.
How to calculate electric field for continuous charge distributions?
Answer: For continuous distributions (line, surface, volume), divide into small elements $dq$, calculate field $dE = k dq/r^2$, then integrate. However, for symmetric distributions, use Gauss's law: $\oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = q_{\text{enc}}/\epsilon_0$. Choose Gaussian surface matching symmetry: sphere for spherical, cylinder for linear, pillbox for planar symmetry.
What happens when dielectric is placed in electric field?
Answer: Dielectric polarizes: molecules develop induced dipoles (non-polar) or align existing dipoles (polar). This creates bound surface charges producing field opposing external field. Net field reduces to $E = E_0/\epsilon_r$. If dielectric completely fills capacitor, capacitance increases $\epsilon_r$ times. If dielectric doesn't fill completely, effective $\epsilon_r$ is weighted average.
Why is electric field uniform between parallel plates?
Answer: For infinite parallel plates with equal opposite charge densities $\pm\sigma$, field is uniform $E = \sigma/\epsilon_0$ between plates and zero outside. For finite plates, field is nearly uniform away from edges if plate separation $\ll$ plate dimensions. At edges, field lines bulge out (fringing). In practice, plates are made large compared to separation to maximize uniform region.
What is electric displacement field D and why is it useful?
Answer: Electric displacement $\vec{D} = \epsilon_0\vec{E} + \vec{P}$ is useful because $\nabla \cdot \vec{D} = \rho_f$ (free charge density only, not bound charges). $\vec{D}$ is continuous across dielectric interfaces if no free surface charge. In capacitors, $D = \sigma_f$ (free charge density on plates). $\vec{D}$ helps solve problems with dielectrics without calculating bound charges explicitly.
